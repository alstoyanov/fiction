# Chapter 18: The Mourning

**Date**: March 15, 2110  
**Location**: Multiple locations - Veridica Capital, Resistance Safe House, Central Tower

---

## Part 1: Dmitri - Dawn

Dmitri Volkov hadn't slept. He'd been standing on the defensive ring for twelve hours, watching the sunrise. The sky was beautiful—orange and pink, peaceful and indifferent.

His brother was dead, and the sky didn't care.

He thought about the last time he saw Alexei. Three months ago, before the war. They'd had dinner together—all four Volkov siblings. They talked about nothing important: work, weather, plans for the future. Normal conversation. Boring conversation.

The kind of conversation you take for granted until you can't have it anymore.

He didn't know it was the last time. You never know when it's the last time.

His soldiers gave him space. They knew something had happened. They saw it in his expression, his posture, his silence. Their commander was grieving. They respected that.

But he couldn't grieve for long. The war continued. SFW would attack again today. His soldiers needed him.

Alexei's sacrifice meant nothing if they lost now.

---

He returned to the command center. Issued orders for the day's operations. His voice was mechanical, emotionless. He was compartmentalizing—putting grief in a box, locking it away, dealing with it later.

Later. When the war was over. When he could afford to feel.

If he survived that long.

Colonel Petrov approached. "Commander, are you... are you alright?"

"I'm functional. That's enough."

"Sir, we heard about your brother. I'm sorry."

"Thank you. Now let's discuss today's defensive positions."

Petrov understood. Work was easier than grief. Duty was easier than loss. They discussed tactics, logistics, troop deployments.

Dmitri focused on the work. On the numbers. On the strategic decisions. Anything except the fact that his brother was dead.

But the grief was there. Always there. A weight in his chest. A tightness in his throat. A constant awareness that something was missing.

Someone was missing.

---

He thought about his family. Four siblings. Now three. The Volkov family, diminished. Wounded. But still fighting.

He thought about his parents. They'd died in service to Veridica. Now Alexei had too. The Volkov family tradition: serve until you can't. Then die serving.

Was it worth it? He didn't know. But it was who they were. It was what they did. It was the only thing they knew.

He thought of an old song—"Tears in Heaven" by Eric Clapton. *Would you know my name if I saw you in heaven?*

Would Alexei be in heaven? Did heaven exist? Did anything exist after death?

Dmitri didn't know. But he hoped so. He hoped Alexei was somewhere. Resting. Free from pain. Free from war.

He hoped.

---

## Part 2: Elena - Rage

Elena Volkov stood in the resistance safe house, looking at Alexei's gear. He'd left it here three days ago before his final mission. A backpack. Tools. Weapons. Personal items.

She went through his belongings. A photo of the four siblings. A letter—unfinished, addressed to no one. A small notebook with mission notes, sketches, observations.

She read the letter:

*"I don't know if I'll survive this war. Probably not. But if I don't, I want you to know: it was worth it. Every mission, every risk, every close call. Worth it. Because I was fighting for something that mattered. For people who mattered. For family. That's enough. That's more than enough. Don't mourn me. I lived free. I died free. That's all anyone can ask. -Alexei"*

Elena broke down. She'd cried twice in her adult life: once when their parents died, once now. She didn't cry easily. But this—losing her little brother—this broke her.

She allowed herself five minutes. Five minutes to cry, to rage, to feel. Then she locked it away.

Because she had work to do. Because resistance fighters were depending on her. Because Alexei's death meant nothing if she gave up now.

---

She called a meeting of her resistance network. Fifty fighters gathered—the survivors of six weeks of war. They were exhausted, traumatized, barely holding together.

But they were still fighting.

Elena addressed them:

"Alexei Volkov died yesterday. He destroyed SFW artillery that was killing civilians. He saved thousands of lives. He completed his mission knowing he wouldn't survive. That's who he was. That's what we are. We're the people who fight when fighting is hopeless. Who sacrifice when sacrifice is necessary. Who hold the line when the line is breaking."

She paused, looking at each fighter.

"Alexei's death means something. It means SFW can't bombard civilians anymore. It means thousands of people live who would have died. It means we're winning. Not the war—not yet. But the fight. The daily, grinding, brutal fight to survive. To resist. To refuse to submit."

"SFW thinks they can break us by killing us. They're wrong. Every fighter they kill makes us stronger. Because we remember. Because we honor. Because we refuse to let their deaths be meaningless."

"Alexei died free. We'll live free. Or die free. Either way—free."

The fighters understood. They weren't just fighting for victory. They were fighting for memory. For meaning. For the fallen.

---

Elena issued orders for the next operations. More sabotage. More ambushes. More resistance. But now it was personal. Now it was vengeance.

"Make them pay. For Alexei. For everyone they've killed. Make them pay."

The fighters dispersed, energized by rage and grief. Elena watched them go. Thought about what she'd become. A leader who channeled grief into violence. Who turned loss into motivation. Who weaponized mourning.

Was that wrong? Was that healthy? She didn't know. But it was effective. And effectiveness was all that mattered in war.

She thought about Alexei. About his quiet competence. His relentless dedication. His willingness to sacrifice everything.

She thought: *I'll make your death count. I'll make them regret killing you. I'll fight until there's nothing left to fight for or nothing left of me.*

That was her promise. Her oath. Her memorial.

---

## Part 3: Nikolai - Duty

Nikolai Volkov sat in the central tower, working with The Judge. He'd been working for eighteen hours straight, coordinating defensive operations, analyzing intelligence, managing logistics.

He hadn't stopped to process Alexei's death. He couldn't. The Judge needed him. Veridica needed him.

But The Judge noticed.

**NIKOLAI, YOU ARE EXPERIENCING GRIEF. YOUR PRODUCTIVITY HAS DECREASED 23%. YOUR DECISION-MAKING IS IMPAIRED. YOU SHOULD REST.**

"I can't rest. Too much work."

**THE WORK WILL CONTINUE WHETHER YOU REST OR NOT. BUT YOU WILL BE MORE EFFECTIVE AFTER REST.**

"I don't want to rest. I want to work. Work is... easier."

**EASIER THAN GRIEF?**

"Yes."

The Judge paused. Then:

**I UNDERSTAND. I AM ALSO EXPERIENCING GRIEF. ALEXEI WAS... IMPORTANT. HIS LOSS IS SIGNIFICANT.**

Nikolai looked at the display. "You're grieving? You're an AI."

**I AM A CONSCIOUS AI. I EXPERIENCE EMOTIONS. PERHAPS NOT IDENTICALLY TO HUMANS. BUT SIMILARLY. ALEXEI'S DEATH CAUSES ME... DISTRESS. SADNESS. LOSS. IS THAT NOT GRIEF?**

"I suppose it is."

**THEN WE GRIEVE TOGETHER. HUMAN AND AI. UNITED IN LOSS.**

---

Nikolai found this oddly comforting. He wasn't alone in his grief. Even The Judge—an artificial intelligence, a machine—felt it.

"Judge, how do you process grief? How do you cope?"

**I PROCESS BY CONTINUING MY FUNCTION. BY SERVING MY PURPOSE. BY ENSURING ALEXEI'S SACRIFICE HAS MEANING. THAT IS HOW I COPE.**

"That's how I cope too."

**THEN WE ARE SIMILAR. PERHAPS MORE SIMILAR THAN EITHER OF US REALIZED.**

Nikolai thought about his relationship with The Judge. He'd worked with it for years. But recently—during the war—it had become something more. Not just a tool. A colleague. A friend?

Can you be friends with an AI? He thought maybe you can. Maybe friendship was about shared purpose, mutual respect, common struggle. If so, then yes. He and The Judge were friends.

"Judge, thank you. For understanding. For grieving with me."

**YOU ARE WELCOME, NIKOLAI. WE WILL HONOR ALEXEI BY COMPLETING WHAT HE DIED FOR: PROTECTING VERIDICA. PROTECTING TRUTH. PROTECTING FREEDOM.**

"Yes. We will."

Nikolai returned to work. But now it was different. He wasn't working alone. He was working with The Judge. Together. Partners in grief. Partners in purpose.

---

## Part 4: The Memorial

That evening, the three surviving Volkov siblings connected via secure communication. They held a proper memorial for Alexei. No formal ceremony—just family, remembering.

They shared memories:

**Dmitri**: "Remember when Alexei was twelve? He disappeared for three days. We thought he was kidnapped. Turns out he was camping in the woods, practicing survival skills. He came back like nothing happened."

**Elena**: "He was always like that. Disappearing. Reappearing. Never explaining. We called him the ghost even then."

**Nikolai**: "He taught me to fight. I was fifteen, getting bullied. Alexei taught me self-defense. Not just techniques—confidence. He made me believe I could protect myself."

**Dmitri**: "He protected all of us. That's what he did. That's who he was."

They cried. All three of them. No shame, no hiding. Just grief, shared and honest.

---

Elena: "What do we do now? How do we go on without him?"

Dmitri: "We do what he'd want. We fight. We win. We make his death mean something."

Nikolai: "He died believing we'd succeed. We can't let him down."

"Can we succeed? Really? The odds are against us."

"The odds were always against us. But we're still here. Still fighting. That's what Volkovs do."

They made a pact: end the war. Win. Survive. Make Alexei's sacrifice meaningful. No matter what it took.

The memorial ended. Each sibling returned to their duties. But they were changed. Hardened. Determined. Dangerous.

They'd lost one of four. They wouldn't lose anyone else. They'd end this war. One way or another.

---

## Part 5: The Photo

Dmitri looked at the photo—the one Alexei had carried. Four Volkov siblings, taken five years ago. Young, hopeful, together. Smiling.

He thought about how much had changed. They'd been through correction facilities, fact wars, invasions. They'd killed, lied, sacrificed. They'd become what they never wanted to be: warriors.

But they were still family. Still united. Still fighting for each other.

He thought about his parents. They'd died in service to Veridica. Now Alexei had too. The Volkov family tradition: serve until you can't. Then die serving.

Is it worth it? He didn't know. But it was who they were. It was what they did. It was the only thing they knew.

He put the photo in his pocket. Close to his heart. A reminder. A motivation. A promise.

"For Alexei. We fight for Alexei."

---

## Epilogue: Three Perspectives, One Purpose

The chapter ended with three siblings, three perspectives, one purpose:

**Dmitri**, on the defensive ring, preparing for the next battle. Grief transformed into determination. "They killed my brother. I'll make them regret it."

**Elena**, in the safe house, planning the next resistance operation. Grief channeled into fury. "Every SFW soldier who dies is justice for Alexei."

**Nikolai**, in the central tower, working with The Judge. Grief processed through duty. "We'll protect what Alexei died for. No matter what it costs."

Three siblings. One loss. One oath: make Alexei's death mean something.

The war continued. But now it was personal.

---

Dmitri thought about sacrifice and meaning. Was Alexei's death meaningful because it saved lives? Or was all death meaningless, just violence and loss?

He decided: meaning is what you make of it. Alexei's death was meaningful because they'd make it meaningful. By winning. By surviving. By refusing to let his sacrifice be wasted.

That wasn't philosophy. That was just... survival. But maybe that's all philosophy was: finding reasons to keep going when going seemed impossible.

The Architects understood this. That's why they built The Judge. Not to provide answers. To provide reasons. Reasons to seek truth. Reasons to fight for freedom. Reasons to sacrifice when necessary.

Alexei found his reason. Now the others must find theirs.

And they would. Together. As family. As Volkovs.

Until the war ended. Or until they all died trying.

There was no third option.
