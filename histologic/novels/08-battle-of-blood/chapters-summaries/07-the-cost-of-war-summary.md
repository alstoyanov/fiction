# Chapter 7: The Cost of War

**Date**: February 15, 2110  
**Location**: Veridica, Field Hospital  
**POV**: Dr. Sarah Chen  
**Word Count Target**: ~3,500 words

## Summary

Dr. Sarah Chen stands in a field hospital, surrounded by wounded soldiers. The smell of blood and antiseptic fills the air. The sounds of pain echo through the tent. This is war, stripped of glory and propaganda. This is what war actually looks like.

She's 38 years old, a trauma surgeon who volunteered for military service when the war started. She thought she was prepared. She'd worked in emergency rooms, seen gunshot wounds, treated accident victims. But nothing prepared her for this.

The wounded arrive in waves. Some are treatable—broken bones, shrapnel wounds, burns. Some are dying—massive trauma, internal bleeding, catastrophic injuries. Some are already dead, but no one's had time to move them yet.

She works for eighteen hours straight. Surgery after surgery. Save this one. Lose that one. Save this one. Lose that one. It becomes mechanical, automatic. She stops seeing people and starts seeing problems to solve.

A young soldier—maybe 20 years old—with his leg blown off. She amputates, cauterizes, stabilizes. He'll live. But he'll never walk normally again.

An older soldier—maybe 40—with shrapnel in his chest. She operates, removes the fragments, repairs the damage. But his lung is destroyed. He'll breathe, but he'll never run again.

A woman soldier—maybe 25—with burns covering 60% of her body. Sarah does what she can, but it's not enough. The woman dies on the operating table. Sarah moves to the next patient.

This is the cost of war. Not the strategic cost, not the political cost. The human cost. The blood cost.

Between surgeries, Sarah talks with other medical staff. They're exhausted, traumatized, barely holding together. One nurse—a young man named Pavel—breaks down crying. "I can't do this anymore. I can't watch them die."

Sarah: "You can. Because if you don't, more will die. We're all they have."

"But it doesn't stop. They keep coming. More and more and more."

"I know. But we keep working. That's all we can do."

It's not comforting. But it's true.

Sarah thinks about her life before the war. She was a successful surgeon in the capital. Good salary, comfortable apartment, respected career. She could have stayed there, treated civilian casualties, avoided the front lines.

But she volunteered. Because someone had to. Because these soldiers needed help. Because she couldn't live with herself if she stayed safe while others suffered.

Now she questions that choice. Not because she regrets helping—but because she's not sure she's strong enough for this.

A soldier arrives—Dmitri Volkov. She recognizes him from the news: war hero, front-line commander, brother of the president. He's wounded—shrapnel in his shoulder, not life-threatening but painful.

She treats him personally. Removes the shrapnel, cleans the wound, bandages it. He's stoic, barely reacting to the pain.

Sarah: "You're lucky. A few centimeters to the left and this would have hit your lung."

Dmitri: "Luck is relative in war."

"You should take leave. Rest. Recover."

"Can't. My soldiers need me."

"Your soldiers need you alive. If you push too hard, you'll get yourself killed."

"Maybe. But if I don't push hard enough, they'll get killed. I'll take my chances."

Sarah understands. It's the same logic that keeps her working eighteen-hour shifts. Someone has to. Might as well be us.

She asks him: "Commander, is it worth it? All this suffering, all these deaths—is it worth it?"

Dmitri thinks before answering: "I don't know. Ask me when the war is over. If we win, maybe it was worth it. If we lose... then all this suffering was for nothing."

"That's not comforting."

"War isn't comforting. It's just necessary."

After Dmitri leaves, Sarah continues working. More wounded arrive. More surgeries. More deaths.

She thinks about the philosophy of war. She's read the old texts: Sun Tzu, Clausewitz, Tolstoy. They all talk about strategy, tactics, necessity. But they don't talk about this—the blood, the pain, the endless stream of broken bodies.

War in theory is about nations, ideologies, strategic objectives. War in practice is about young people dying in pain, far from home, for reasons they barely understand.

She wonders: do the leaders who start wars ever see this? Do they ever stand in field hospitals, watching soldiers die? Or do they just see maps, statistics, strategic positions?

She thinks of an old anti-war song: "War Pigs" by Black Sabbath. "Politicians hide themselves away / They only started the war / Why should they go out to fight? / They leave that role to the poor."

It's true. President Volkov isn't here. General Sokolov isn't here. They're in command centers, making strategic decisions. She's here, dealing with the consequences.

But then she thinks: that's not fair. They carry different burdens. Sokolov has to decide which soldiers to send to die. Volkov has to decide whether the war is worth fighting at all. Those are terrible choices too.

Everyone in war carries a burden. Hers is just more visceral.

That night—or maybe it's day, she's lost track—Sarah takes a break. She steps outside the hospital tent, breathes fresh air, tries to clear her head.

She sees the night sky. Stars. Beautiful, peaceful, indifferent. The universe doesn't care about human wars. The stars don't care who wins or loses. They just shine.

It's oddly comforting. War feels all-consuming when you're in it. But from the stars' perspective, it's nothing. A brief moment of violence on a small planet. Insignificant.

She thinks: maybe that's the right perspective. War matters to us because we're in it. But in the grand scheme, it's just... temporary. This will end. Eventually. One way or another.

She goes back inside. More wounded have arrived. She returns to work.

The chapter ends with Sarah performing another surgery. A young soldier, barely 18, with a chest wound. She works carefully, methodically, trying to save him.

She succeeds. The soldier will live. One more life saved. One small victory against death.

She thinks: this is why she does it. Not for glory, not for recognition. Just for this—one more person who gets to live. One more family that doesn't receive a death notice. One more soldier who goes home.

It's not much. But it's something. And in war, something is everything.
