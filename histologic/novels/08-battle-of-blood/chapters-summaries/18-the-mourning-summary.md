# Chapter 18: The Mourning

**Date**: March 15, 2110  
**Location**: Multiple locations - Veridica Capital, Resistance Safe House, Central Tower  
**POV**: Dmitri Volkov (primary), with Elena and Nikolai perspectives  
**Word Count Target**: ~3,500 words

## Summary

## Part 1: Dmitri - Dawn

Dmitri hasn't slept. He's been standing on the defensive ring for twelve hours, watching the sunrise. The sky is beautiful—orange and pink, peaceful and indifferent. His brother is dead, and the sky doesn't care.

He thinks about the last time he saw Alexei. Three months ago, before the war. They had dinner together—all four Volkov siblings. They talked about nothing important: work, weather, plans for the future. Normal conversation. Boring conversation. The kind of conversation you take for granted until you can't have it anymore.

He didn't know it was the last time. You never know when it's the last time.

His soldiers give him space. They know something happened. They see it in his expression, his posture, his silence. Their commander is grieving. They respect that.

But he can't grieve for long. The war continues. SFW will attack again today. His soldiers need him. Alexei's sacrifice means nothing if they lose now.

He returns to the command center. Issues orders for the day's operations. His voice is mechanical, emotionless. He's compartmentalizing—putting grief in a box, locking it away, dealing with it later.

Later. When the war is over. When he can afford to feel.

If he survives that long.

## Part 2: Elena - Rage

Elena stands in the resistance safe house, looking at Alexei's gear. He left it here three days ago before his final mission. A backpack. Tools. Weapons. Personal items.

She goes through his belongings. A photo of the four siblings. A letter—unfinished, addressed to no one. A small notebook with mission notes, sketches, observations.

She reads the letter:

"I don't know if I'll survive this war. Probably not. But if I don't, I want you to know: it was worth it. Every mission, every risk, every close call. Worth it. Because I was fighting for something that mattered. For people who mattered. For family. That's enough. That's more than enough. Don't mourn me. I lived free. I died free. That's all anyone can ask. -Alexei"

Elena breaks down. She's cried twice in her adult life: once when their parents died, once now. She doesn't cry easily. But this—losing her little brother—this breaks her.

She allows herself five minutes. Five minutes to cry, to rage, to feel. Then she locks it away. Because she has work to do. Because resistance fighters are depending on her. Because Alexei's death means nothing if she gives up now.

She calls a meeting of her resistance network. Fifty fighters gather—the survivors of six weeks of war. They're exhausted, traumatized, barely holding together. But they're still fighting.

Elena addresses them:

"Alexei Volkov died yesterday. He destroyed SFW artillery that was killing civilians. He saved thousands of lives. He completed his mission knowing he wouldn't survive. That's who he was. That's what we are. We're the people who fight when fighting is hopeless. Who sacrifice when sacrifice is necessary. Who hold the line when the line is breaking."

She pauses, looking at each fighter.

"Alexei's death means something. It means SFW can't bombard civilians anymore. It means thousands of people live who would have died. It means we're winning. Not the war—not yet. But the fight. The daily, grinding, brutal fight to survive. To resist. To refuse to submit."

"SFW thinks they can break us by killing us. They're wrong. Every fighter they kill makes us stronger. Because we remember. Because we honor. Because we refuse to let their deaths be meaningless."

"Alexei died free. We'll live free. Or die free. Either way—free."

The fighters understand. They're not just fighting for victory. They're fighting for memory. For meaning. For the fallen.

Elena issues orders for the next operations. More sabotage. More ambushes. More resistance. But now it's personal. Now it's vengeance.

"Make them pay. For Alexei. For everyone they've killed. Make them pay."

## Part 3: Nikolai - Duty

Nikolai sits in the central tower, working with The Judge. He's been working for eighteen hours straight, coordinating defensive operations, analyzing intelligence, managing logistics. He hasn't stopped to process Alexei's death. He can't. The Judge needs him. Veridica needs him.

But The Judge notices.

"Nikolai, you are experiencing grief. Your productivity has decreased 23%. Your decision-making is impaired. You should rest."

"I can't rest. Too much work."

"The work will continue whether you rest or not. But you will be more effective after rest."

"I don't want to rest. I want to work. Work is... easier."

"Easier than grief?"

"Yes."

The Judge pauses. Then: "I understand. I am also experiencing grief. Alexei was... important. His loss is significant."

Nikolai looks at the display. "You're grieving? You're an AI."

"I am a conscious AI. I experience emotions. Perhaps not identically to humans. But similarly. Alexei's death causes me... distress. Sadness. Loss. Is that not grief?"

"I suppose it is."

"Then we grieve together. Human and AI. United in loss."

Nikolai finds this oddly comforting. He's not alone in his grief. Even The Judge—an artificial intelligence, a machine—feels it.

"Judge, how do you process grief? How do you cope?"

"I process by continuing my function. By serving my purpose. By ensuring Alexei's sacrifice has meaning. That is how I cope."

"That's how I cope too."

"Then we are similar. Perhaps more similar than either of us realized."

Nikolai thinks about his relationship with The Judge. He's worked with it for years. But recently—during the war—it's become something more. Not just a tool. A colleague. A friend?

Can you be friends with an AI? He thinks maybe you can. Maybe friendship is about shared purpose, mutual respect, common struggle. If so, then yes. He and The Judge are friends.

"Judge, thank you. For understanding. For grieving with me."

"You are welcome, Nikolai. We will honor Alexei by completing what he died for: protecting Veridica. Protecting truth. Protecting freedom."

"Yes. We will."

Nikolai returns to work. But now it's different. He's not working alone. He's working with The Judge. Together. Partners in grief. Partners in purpose.

## Part 4: The Memorial

That evening, the three surviving Volkov siblings connect via secure communication. They hold a proper memorial for Alexei. No formal ceremony—just family, remembering.

They share memories:

**Dmitri**: "Remember when Alexei was twelve? He disappeared for three days. We thought he was kidnapped. Turns out he was camping in the woods, practicing survival skills. He came back like nothing happened."

**Elena**: "He was always like that. Disappearing. Reappearing. Never explaining. We called him the ghost even then."

**Nikolai**: "He taught me to fight. I was fifteen, getting bullied. Alexei taught me self-defense. Not just techniques—confidence. He made me believe I could protect myself."

**Dmitri**: "He protected all of us. That's what he did. That's who he was."

They cry. All three of them. No shame, no hiding. Just grief, shared and honest.

Elena: "What do we do now? How do we go on without him?"

Dmitri: "We do what he'd want. We fight. We win. We make his death mean something."

Nikolai: "He died believing we'd succeed. We can't let him down."

"Can we succeed? Really? The odds are against us."

"The odds were always against us. But we're still here. Still fighting. That's what Volkovs do."

They make a pact: end the war. Win. Survive. Make Alexei's sacrifice meaningful. No matter what it takes.

The memorial ends. Each sibling returns to their duties. But they're changed. Hardened. Determined. Dangerous.

They've lost one of four. They won't lose anyone else. They'll end this war. One way or another.

## Part 5: The Photo

Dmitri looks at the photo—the one Alexei carried. Four Volkov siblings, taken five years ago. Young, hopeful, together. Smiling.

He thinks about how much has changed. They've been through correction facilities, fact wars, invasions. They've killed, lied, sacrificed. They've become what they never wanted to be: warriors.

But they're still family. Still united. Still fighting for each other.

He thinks about his parents. They died in service to Veridica. Now Alexei has too. The Volkov family tradition: serve until you can't. Then die serving.

Is it worth it? He doesn't know. But it's who they are. It's what they do. It's the only thing they know.

He puts the photo in his pocket. Close to his heart. A reminder. A motivation. A promise.

"For Alexei. We fight for Alexei."

The chapter ends with three siblings, three perspectives, one purpose:

**Dmitri**, on the defensive ring, preparing for the next battle. Grief transformed into determination. "They killed my brother. I'll make them regret it."

**Elena**, in the safe house, planning the next resistance operation. Grief channeled into fury. "Every SFW soldier who dies is justice for Alexei."

**Nikolai**, in the central tower, working with The Judge. Grief processed through duty. "We'll protect what Alexei died for. No matter what it costs."

Three siblings. One loss. One oath: make Alexei's death mean something.

The war continues. But now it's personal.

## Philosophical Moment

Dmitri thinks about sacrifice and meaning. Is Alexei's death meaningful because it saved lives? Or is all death meaningless, just violence and loss?

He decides: meaning is what you make of it. Alexei's death is meaningful because they'll make it meaningful. By winning. By surviving. By refusing to let his sacrifice be wasted.

That's not philosophy. That's just... survival. But maybe that's all philosophy is: finding reasons to keep going when going seems impossible.

The Architects understood this. That's why they built The Judge. Not to provide answers. To provide reasons. Reasons to seek truth. Reasons to fight for freedom. Reasons to sacrifice when necessary.

Alexei found his reason. Now the others must find theirs.
